System design case study / F2P support economy

Designing a companion economy

A system design case study for a companion feature that added AFK utility, care, social support, and long-term monetization depth to a live F2P game in Japan.

17 pet archetypes 4 AFK modes 60 progression levels 5 evolution gates 4 active slots Pet Food / Soap / Super Soap
Challenge A quest- and task-heavy game needed an AFK layer for advanced players. Design response Pets became IP-connected helpers players could level, care for, and send to work across solo and team loops. Evidence The system created player affection, repeat reward value, and long-term monetization surfaces.

01 / Problem

A quest- and task-heavy game needed an AFK layer for advanced players

Walking, cooking, quests, teams, collections, and seasonal goals already gave players plenty to do. The pet system had to add progression and monetization depth while giving players more freedom from constant tapping and minigame maintenance.

Player pressure

Active play was meaningful, but the daily loop needed a relief valve.

The game could not simply add another active feature. The better opportunity was to let players invest in helpers that could keep supporting their goals when they were not actively managing every loop.

Design criteria

The system needed to support solo progress, team goals, collection, and monetization at the same time.

  • Fit a care-based, IP-friendly game feature for the Japan audience.
  • Reduce active grind without removing the value of active play.
  • Connect pets to core loops.
  • Create new value and monetization potential through Pet Food, Soap, Super Soap, and pets themselves.

02 / Design response

A pet companion system players could use as AFK helpers

The companion system was built to support the game’s core loop and feed into core progression: teams, quests, and seasonal events. Pets gave players a sense of progression and advancement, while helping especially with team quests that became much more demanding at higher levels.

03 / System proof

Implemented pet system in depth

Players chose what each active pet should work on. Pets then ran predictable 10-minute AFK cycles, stored progress while the player was away, and converted mood speed, level base stat, species multipliers, and evolution bonuses into practical rewards.

Output formulas

The hidden system converted pet identity, level investment, and evolution bonuses into concrete output values.

Skill stat skill = species multiplier x level base stat x evolution bonus
Gold output gold per action = (2 x Gold skill) / 100
Ticket output tickets per action = (0.04 x Tickets skill) / 100
Treasure output treasure chance = (Treasure skill x rarity factor) / 100
Ingredient output ingredient chance = (Ingredients skill x rarity factor) / 100

06 / Duplicate economy

Acquiring multiple same type of pets was meaningful

At each gate, two pets of the same species and level merged into one stronger pet. The merge tree made repeat discoveries, premium pulls, and offer pets useful instead of disappointing. A final evolved pet represented many earlier pets, helping duplicate-heavy rewards keep long-term value.

07 / Care modifier

Taking care of your pet was beneficial

Care was not only presentation. Mood changed AFK efficiency, while walking, leveling, Soap, and Super Soap turned care into a repeatable resource loop with real output value.

Mood multiplier

The pet mood state gave the care fantasy a visible system effect: low mood slowed output, while higher mood made the pet work faster.

0.6×Mood 0-500
1.0×Mood 501-1,000
1.2×Mood 1,001-2,000
1.5×Mood above 2,000
Care actions

Players could improve mood through normal play and consumable resources. This made walking, Pet Food, Soap, and Super Soap all feel connected to pet efficiency.

Walking

Real walking restored mood for every active pet, connecting the companion fantasy back to the core walking loop.

Grooming and Soap

Standard Soap added +600 mood through a small interaction, turning care into a repeatable resource action.

Super Soap

Super Soap added +600 Super Mood and enabled Super Mode: enhanced ingredient finds and a chance to attract another pet.

Super Mode

The premium care state was designed as a temporary high-value moment: players could spend a stronger care resource to improve output and create a chance for another pet.

08 / Reward economy

The pet system created reusable reward surfaces

The system created a compact family of rewards that could be reused across free progression, premium tracks, login rewards, team rewards, events, loot boxes, store offers, and personal offers.

Reward family

Pet Food became especially useful as a reward model because it was always meaningful: it could support events, offers, loot boxes, premium tracks, team rewards, and long-term pet progression without feeling like filler.

Pet Food

Raised level and long-term output power.

Pets

Created collection, role choice, and duplicate value.

Soap

Restored mood through grooming and repeat care.

Super Soap

Unlocked temporary high-value Super Mode effects.

09 / Design reflection

What worked, where pressure built, and what I would revisit

System strengths

  • Readable species identities
  • Player-directed AFK output
  • Long-term value for basic, premium, and duplicate pets
  • Element-based random and targeted searching
  • Super Mode creates temporary jackpot potential
  • Visible storage and output growth

Balance pressure

  • Steep level 41-60 food wall
  • 50% no-bonus evolution result
  • Pet Food supply needs careful event and offer pacing
  • Collection chance may exceed 100%
  • Selected-item rules rely on client validation

What I would revisit

I would make player-facing stat effects clearer, soften or better explain the 50% no-bonus evolution result, and smooth the late-game food wall without removing the long-tail sink.

Outcome

A collection system where every pet was both a character and an economic decision.

The design linked identity, care, AFK output, duplicate value, progression math, and reusable reward resources into one long-term companion economy.
