Case study / Delivery under constraint

Shipping base building in 60 man-days

I was tasked to deliver a functional 3D base-building feature for Moomin Move on a fixed 60 man-day budget. I sequenced the work so development never waited on art, hit the deadline with a playable MVP, and the feature later grew into a core part of the game.

60 man-day budget Shipped into a live game 3D over 2D MVP, then expanded Design · UX · monetization Environment & optimization

01 / The brief

A functional base-building game, in a fixed budget

The ask was concrete: add a working base-building feature to an already-live game within 60 man-days. The game was in service with real players, so the work had to integrate cleanly without disruption. The real challenge was scoping and sequencing, not just designing.

The constraint

60 man-days to a playable, functional feature.

A fixed budget inside a live F2P game meant the plan had to protect development time, reuse where possible, and reach a testable build fast rather than chase polish first.

My role

I owned the experience, the world, and the plan.

  • Game design and monetization design for the feature
  • UI and UX for build, craft, and grid placement
  • Visual environment design and greyboxing
  • Textures, skybox, and scene optimization
  • Stakeholder communication and scope decisions

A dedicated prop artist made the furniture, and a code developer built the systems. I connected design, art, monetization, and delivery.

02 / First decision

2D or 3D, decided with quick visual tests

Before committing the budget, I pitched the format. I produced fast visual tests of how a build space could look in the Moomin world so the team could choose with their eyes, not just a spec. 3D was selected for its depth and decoration value.

03 / Sequencing

Unblock the developer on day one

To protect the 60-day budget, I made the work parallel from the start. Instead of building a finished world, I built the smallest possible one and handed it over immediately.

Move 01

Smallest playable world first

I created just a flat surface with the Moomin house in the center, so the developer could start at once on camera controls and a crude grid-placement system. Placeholder furniture went in early, and we tested controls and placement before any real art existed. Development never waited on me.

04 / Greybox

I gray-boxed the first world myself

While controls matured in parallel, I blocked out the first full world in grey geometry. It was the fast way to lock layout, scale, and composition without waiting on final art, and it gave the team a real space to test against.

05 / Polish & optimization

Textured, optimized, and finished on mobile

With layout locked, I fine-tuned and textured the environment, then made it cheap to run. Simple tiling textures, a skybox, and linear fog hid the horizon without cost, and the UI was refined into working crafting and grid-placement systems.

Environment craft

Tiling textures and a faked horizon

I authored the environment textures myself: simple tiling grass, path, and water, plus a painted skybox and linear fog to hide the horizon at almost no cost. Cheap to render, and easy to vary by season later.

Years of level design, applied to speed

I leaned on years of level design and environment-optimization experience to build and tune the world quickly and keep it highly performant on mobile, which is what let a full 3D scene ship inside the budget.

06 / The 60-day result

A playable MVP, delivered on time

At 60 man-days the feature was functional and shipping: a 3D valley you could build, craft, and decorate. The prop artist's first furniture set became sellable items in loot boxes alongside craftable pieces, giving the feature day-one economy hooks.

07 / After validation

From a visual minigame to the core of the game

At launch it was deliberately small, a light visual minigame rather than core gameplay. Over the next two years, two major updates changed that. The latest brought base building to the very heart of the game, making it a meaningful part of the monetization and retention gains in that period.

What the expansion added

Valley visitors, party mode, and gifting in the valley, plus a recurring build event. Onboarding now starts from the valley, and tasks and story were reframed around cleaning and building it for friends in need, making the valley a reason to return rather than a side feature.

Repositioned as the heart of progression

In the latest updates the valley became the most important part of the game's visual progression: a safe space for the friends you meet along the way, and a place to celebrate and share what you have built. I led that design change, taking it from a visual prototype to an integral part of the experience.

08 / Reflection

What I owned, what worked, and what it taught

What I owned

  • Game and monetization design
  • UI and UX for build, craft, and placement
  • Visual environment design and greyboxing
  • Textures, skybox, and scene optimization
  • Stakeholder communication and scope

Prop art and code were separate roles; I tied the disciplines together.

What worked

  • Smallest-world-first kept the developer busy from day one
  • Greybox before art locked scope fast
  • Years of level-design experience let 3D ship on mobile
  • An MVP that was validated, then expanded

What it taught

A hard budget is a design tool. I shipped the cheapest testable version first to protect the schedule, while holding the larger vision the whole time. When the feature earned the investment, I led the design change that made the valley a core, social heart of the game. Delivery discipline first, vision realized once it was earned.

Outcome

A 60 man-day feature that became part of the game's core.

Scoping, smart sequencing, and mobile optimization delivered a functional 3D base-building feature on a fixed budget, and a validated base that grew into social, seasonal, and onboarding gameplay.
